# Foxbridge Tort Act

*A statutory restatement of the law of torts in England-on-Polis: civil wrongs giving rise to damages or injunction. Adapted from English common-law tort principles plus statutory adjuncts (UK Civil Liability Acts, OGL v3.0). Substrate-fictionalised per `polis/configs/sources.yaml`. The Foxbridge Defamation Act sits in `laws/press/defamation-and-libel.md` rather than here, but is conceptually a tort.*

---

## Part 1 — General principles

### §1 What is a tort

A tort is a civil wrong, independent of contract, that gives rise to a claim for damages, injunction, or other remedy. The aim is **compensation** of the claimant rather than punishment of the defendant; punitive damages are exceptional.

### §2 Required elements

A tort claim generally requires the claimant to establish:
  (a) a duty owed by the defendant;
  (b) breach of that duty;
  (c) causation (factual and legal) between breach and harm;
  (d) recoverable harm (not too remote, mitigated where reasonable).

## Part 2 — Negligence

### §3 Duty of care

(1) A duty of care is owed where the relationship between the parties satisfies the **Caparo-equivalent** test:
  (a) reasonable foreseeability of harm to the claimant;
  (b) sufficient proximity between claimant and defendant;
  (c) it is fair, just, and reasonable to impose a duty.

(2) Specific duty-of-care doctrines apply to:
  (a) road users to other road users and pedestrians;
  (b) employers to employees (Foxbridge Employment Act §10 enriched by negligence);
  (c) occupiers to visitors and (limited) trespassers (Foxbridge Property Act §8);
  (d) professionals to clients (medical, legal, accounting, financial-advisory);
  (e) **football clubs to players** for safe training and match conditions;
  (f) public bodies in defined circumstances (the Hill-equivalent and X-equivalent doctrines).

### §4 Breach

(1) The defendant is in breach if their conduct falls below the standard of the reasonable person in the relevant circumstances.

(2) **Professional defendants** are judged by the standard of a reasonably competent member of their profession. The Bolam-equivalent test (acceptance of practice as reasonable by a responsible body of professional opinion) modified by Bolitho-equivalent (the practice must withstand logical scrutiny).

(3) For amateur and recreational defendants the standard is that of the reasonable participant.

### §5 Causation

(1) **Factual causation**: the "but for" test — would the harm have occurred but for the breach?

(2) **Legal causation**: was the harm a foreseeable consequence of the type the duty was designed to guard against, and not too remote?

(3) **Multiple-cause cases**: material-contribution, loss-of-chance, and indivisible-injury doctrines apply.

### §6 Damage

(1) Recoverable harms include:
  (a) personal injury and consequential losses (medical expenses, lost earnings, loss of amenity);
  (b) property damage and consequential losses;
  (c) pure economic loss (limited to specific categories: negligent misstatement, professional services, *Hedley-Byrne*-equivalent);
  (d) psychiatric injury (recognised mental illness; "primary" and "secondary" victim categories apply).

(2) Mere distress, disappointment, or upset short of recognised psychiatric injury is not a recoverable head of damage in negligence.

## Part 3 — Specific torts

### §7 Trespass

(1) **Trespass to land**: direct, intentional intrusion onto another's land without permission.

(2) **Trespass to person**: assault (intentional creation of apprehension of immediate harmful contact), battery (intentional harmful or offensive contact), and false imprisonment (intentional total restraint of movement).

(3) **Trespass to goods**: direct, intentional interference with another's chattels.

### §8 Nuisance

(1) **Private nuisance**: substantial and unreasonable interference with another's use or enjoyment of land. Cross-references Foxbridge Property Act §7.

(2) **Public nuisance**: act or omission obstructing or causing damage to the public's exercise of common rights, actionable by the Attorney General of Foxbridge or by individuals suffering particular damage beyond the public at large.

### §9 Defamation

Cross-reference: Foxbridge Defamation Act (`laws/press/defamation-and-libel.md`). The serious-harm threshold (§1) and defences (§§2–4) live there.

### §10 Conversion

The intentional dealing with another's chattel in a manner inconsistent with their right of possession, including taking, withholding, destroying, or unauthorised disposing.

### §11 Deceit

A false representation made with knowledge of falsity (or recklessly), intending the claimant to rely on it, where the claimant did rely and suffered loss as a result.

### §12 Conspiracy

(1) **Lawful-means conspiracy**: two or more persons combining with the predominant purpose of injuring the claimant, by means lawful in themselves but predominantly intended to injure.

(2) **Unlawful-means conspiracy**: combination using unlawful means with intention to injure.

### §13 Inducing breach of contract

Knowingly inducing a third party to break their contract with the claimant, causing loss.

### §14 Passing off

Misrepresenting one's goods, services, or business as connected with the claimant's, where the claimant has goodwill in the relevant identifying features and the misrepresentation causes damage to that goodwill.

### §15 Sport-specific torts

(1) **Negligence on the field of play**: applies to participants whose conduct falls below that of a reasonably competent participant in the relevant sport. The standard is informed by the rules of the game and accepted practices, not pure ordinary-life standards.

(2) **Negligent refereeing**: a referee owes a duty of care to participants for their physical safety and may be liable for serious failures (e.g., not abandoning play in unsafe conditions).

(3) **Stadium negligence**: stadium operators owe duties under Foxbridge Football Act §12 stadium-safety regime plus this Part.

(4) **Image-rights infringement**: the unauthorised commercial use of a footballer's identifying features (likeness, name, distinctive number) is actionable in passing off (§14) and via the Foxbridge Intellectual Property Act provisions on personality rights.

## Part 4 — Defences

### §16 Consent

Voluntary consent (volenti non fit injuria) bars or limits a tort claim where the claimant freely agreed to bear the risk. Consent is implied in some sport contexts: a footballer impliedly consents to ordinary tackling within the rules but not to deliberate violent conduct.

### §17 Contributory negligence

Where the claimant is partly responsible for their loss, damages are reduced by the just-and-equitable share. Contributory negligence does not extinguish the claim.

### §18 Necessity, self-defence, illegality

(1) Acts done out of necessity to prevent greater harm may not constitute a tort.

(2) Self-defence justifies proportionate force against a threat to person or property.

(3) The Foxbridge ex turpi causa rule may bar a claim founded on the claimant's own serious illegality.

## Part 5 — Remedies

### §19 Damages

(1) **Compensatory damages** restore the claimant to the position they would have been in had the tort not occurred (so far as money can do so).

(2) **Aggravated damages** address the manner in which the tort was committed where it has caused special distress.

(3) **Exemplary (punitive) damages** are available only in narrow categories (oppressive conduct by public officials; calculated profit-motivated wrongs).

(4) **Nominal damages** mark a vindication where the wrong is established but no substantial loss is shown.

### §20 Injunction

A prohibitory or mandatory order, available where damages are inadequate. Subject to discretion, balance of convenience, and the nature of the wrong.

### §21 Specific restitution

Available for trespass to goods and conversion: the order to deliver up the chattel.

## Part 6 — Limitation

### §22 Limitation periods

(1) Personal injury: **3 years** from the cause of action accruing or knowledge thereof.

(2) Defamation: **1 year** from publication (with the single-publication rule per Foxbridge Defamation Act §8).

(3) Other tort: **6 years**.

(4) Latent damage and concealment: extended date-of-knowledge periods apply per the Foxbridge Limitation Act.

## Part 7 — Citation, commencement, and extent

### §23 Short title, commencement, and extent

(1) This Act may be cited as the **Foxbridge Tort Act**.

(2) This Act came into force on **2026-08-01** in-world.

(3) This Act extends to England-on-Polis only.
